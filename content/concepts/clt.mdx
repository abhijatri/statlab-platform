## The Sampling Distribution of the Mean

Pick any population with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2 < \infty$.
Draw $n$ independent observations and form their average $\bar{X}_n$.
The CLT says:

$$
\sqrt{n}\,\frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma} \;\xrightarrow{d}\; \mathcal{N}(0,1)
\quad \text{as } n\to\infty
$$

The target distribution does not depend on the shape of the population at all — only on $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$.

<Intuition>
The magic: averaging n independent copies dilutes any individual quirk of the population.
Extreme values average out, skewness flattens, and the only surviving structure is the
mean and variance. Try the Bimodal or Pareto population — they look nothing like a Normal,
but watch what happens to their sample means as n grows.
</Intuition>

## What to Observe

**Uniform\[0, 1\]:** Already nearly symmetric — the histogram looks close to Normal even at $n=2$.

**Exponential($\lambda=1$):** Right-skewed (skewness = 2). You need $n \approx 10$--$25$ before the
histogram loses its right lean.

**Bimodal Mixture:** The two humps are obvious at $n=1$. By $n=5$--$10$, they merge into a single
bell — the central peak of the normal wins.

**Pareto($\alpha=3$):** Finite variance ($\sigma^2=0.75$), but fat right tail. Convergence is
slow because the Berry-Esseen bound depends on the third absolute moment, which is large.

## The Berry-Esseen Bound

The CLT gives the *direction* of convergence; Berry-Esseen gives its *rate*:

$$
\sup_x \left| P\!\left(\frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \le x\right) - \Phi(x) \right|
\;\le\; \frac{C\,\rho}{\sigma^3\,\sqrt{n}}
$$

where $\rho = \mathbb{E}[|X - \mu|^3]$ is the third absolute central moment and $C \le 0.4748$.
Populations with large $\rho/\sigma^3$ (high skewness or fat third moment) need larger $n$.

## When the CLT Fails

The finite-variance condition $\sigma^2 < \infty$ is essential.
For a Pareto with $\alpha \le 2$, the variance is infinite and the sample mean does not
converge to a Normal — it converges to a **stable distribution** instead.
This is why heavy-tailed financial data requires models beyond the CLT.
