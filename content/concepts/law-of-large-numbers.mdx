## Intuition

<Intuition>
Flip a fair coin repeatedly. After 10 flips, the proportion of heads might be far from $\tfrac{1}{2}$.
After $10\,000$ flips, it will be close. After $10^9$ flips, it will be very close.

The Law of Large Numbers makes this precise: the sample average
$\bar{X}_n = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$
stabilises around the population mean $\mu = E[X]$ as $n \to \infty$.

There are two versions. The **Weak Law** says that the probability of being far from $\mu$ tends to zero.
The **Strong Law** says something stronger: with probability one, the sample path
$n \mapsto \bar{X}_n$ itself converges to $\mu$.
The distinction is the difference between "unlikely to be far" and "will eventually stay close forever."
</Intuition>

## Setup

<Definition number={1} name="Sample Mean">
Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be a sequence of random variables on a probability space
$(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$. The **sample mean** of the first $n$ observations is

$$\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i.$$
</Definition>

<Definition number={2} name="i.i.d. Sequence">
A sequence $(X_n)_{n \geq 1}$ is **independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.)** if:
1. The $X_i$ are mutually independent: $P(X_1 \in A_1, \ldots, X_k \in A_k) = \prod P(X_i \in A_i)$.
2. All $X_i$ share the same distribution: $P(X_i \in A) = P(X_1 \in A)$ for all $i$ and Borel $A$.
</Definition>

We recall two modes of convergence that appear in the LLN.

<Definition number={3} name="Convergence in Probability">
A sequence of random variables $(T_n)$ **converges in probability** to $T$, written $T_n \xrightarrow{P} T$, if
$$\forall\,\varepsilon > 0: \quad \lim_{n \to \infty} P\bigl(|T_n - T| > \varepsilon\bigr) = 0.$$
</Definition>

<Definition number={4} name="Almost Sure Convergence">
$(T_n)$ **converges almost surely** to $T$, written $T_n \xrightarrow{\mathrm{a.s.}} T$, if
$$P\!\left(\lim_{n \to \infty} T_n = T\right) = 1,$$
i.e., there exists a null set $N \in \mathcal{F}$ with $P(N) = 0$ such that $T_n(\omega) \to T(\omega)$ for all $\omega \notin N$.
</Definition>

<Theorem number={1} name="Hierarchy of Convergence Modes">
Almost sure convergence implies convergence in probability:
$$T_n \xrightarrow{\mathrm{a.s.}} T \implies T_n \xrightarrow{P} T.$$
The converse is false in general.
</Theorem>

<Proof collapsible defaultOpen={false} label="Proof of Theorem 1">
Fix $\varepsilon > 0$. Define the events $A_n = \{|T_n - T| > \varepsilon\}$.

Almost sure convergence means $P(\limsup_n A_n) = 0$, i.e.,
$P\!\left(\bigcap_{N=1}^\infty \bigcup_{n \geq N} A_n\right) = 0$.

Since $A_n \subseteq \bigcup_{k \geq n} A_k$, we have
$P(A_n) \leq P\!\left(\bigcup_{k \geq n} A_k\right) \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$,
where the last limit follows because $\bigcup_{k \geq n} A_k \downarrow \limsup_n A_n$
and continuity of probability. Thus $P(A_n) \to 0$.
</Proof>

## Weak Law of Large Numbers

<Theorem number={2} name="Weak Law (Chebyshev)">
Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. with $E[X_1] = \mu$ and $\mathrm{Var}(X_1) = \sigma^2 < \infty$.
Then
$$\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{P} \mu.$$
</Theorem>

<Proof label="Proof of Theorem 2">
Since the $X_i$ are i.i.d., the sample mean satisfies $E[\bar{X}_n] = \mu$ and
$$\mathrm{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}.$$

By **Chebyshev's inequality**, for any $\varepsilon > 0$:
$$P\!\left(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| > \varepsilon\right) \leq \frac{\mathrm{Var}(\bar{X}_n)}{\varepsilon^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{n\varepsilon^2} \xrightarrow{n \to \infty} 0.$$
</Proof>

<Warning title="Finite Variance is Not Necessary">
The Chebyshev proof uses $\mathrm{Var}(X) < \infty$, but this is an artifact of the proof technique,
not a requirement of the result. The WLLN holds under the weaker condition $E|X| < \infty$.
The proof in that generality uses **characteristic functions** (Theorem 3).
</Warning>

<Theorem number={3} name="Weak Law (Khinchin)">
Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. with $E[X_1] = \mu$ (finite mean, no variance assumption).
Then $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{P} \mu$.
</Theorem>

<Proof collapsible defaultOpen={false} label="Proof via Characteristic Functions">
Let $\varphi(t) = E[e^{itX_1}]$ be the characteristic function of $X_1$.
Since $E|X_1| < \infty$, $\varphi$ is differentiable and $\varphi'(0) = i\mu$.

The characteristic function of $\bar{X}_n$ is
$$\varphi_{\bar{X}_n}(t) = E\!\left[\exp\!\left(it\bar{X}_n\right)\right] = \prod_{i=1}^n E\!\left[\exp\!\left(\frac{it}{n}X_i\right)\right] = \left[\varphi\!\left(\frac{t}{n}\right)\right]^n.$$

For fixed $t$, as $n \to \infty$:
$$\log \varphi\!\left(\frac{t}{n}\right) = \log\!\left(1 + i\mu\frac{t}{n} + o\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right) = i\mu\frac{t}{n} + o\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right).$$

Hence $n \log \varphi(t/n) \to i\mu t$, so
$$\varphi_{\bar{X}_n}(t) = \left[\varphi\!\left(\frac{t}{n}\right)\right]^n \to e^{i\mu t}.$$

The function $e^{i\mu t}$ is the characteristic function of the point mass $\delta_\mu$.
By **Lévy's continuity theorem**, $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{d} \mu$ (convergence in distribution to a constant).
Convergence in distribution to a constant is equivalent to convergence in probability.
</Proof>

## Strong Law of Large Numbers

<Theorem number={4} name="Strong Law (Kolmogorov)">
Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. with $E|X_1| < \infty$ and $E[X_1] = \mu$.
Then
$$\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{\mathrm{a.s.}} \mu.$$
</Theorem>

The full proof of Kolmogorov's SLLN is more involved than the WLLN. We present the key ideas.

<Proof collapsible defaultOpen={false} label="Proof Sketch (Truncation + Borel-Cantelli)">
**Step 1: Truncation.** Define $Y_k = X_k \mathbf{1}_{\{|X_k| \leq k\}}$.
Since $E|X_1| < \infty$, the truncated and original means have the same a.s. limit:
one can show $\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^n (X_k - Y_k) \to 0$ a.s. using the first Borel-Cantelli lemma and the condition $\sum_{k=1}^\infty P(|X_1| > k) < \infty$ (which is equivalent to $E|X_1| < \infty$).

**Step 2: Convergence of the truncated series.** For the truncated variables,
$E[Y_k] \to \mu$ by dominated convergence. By the **Kronecker lemma**, it suffices to show
$$\sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{\mathrm{Var}(Y_k)}{k^2} < \infty.$$

This bound follows from
$\sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{E[Y_k^2]}{k^2} \leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{1}{k^2} \int_0^k 2x\, P(|X_1| > x)\,dx$,
which converges whenever $E|X_1| < \infty$ (by a Fubini-type exchange of summation and integration).

**Conclusion.** Combining the two steps, $\bar{X}_n \to \mu$ a.s.
</Proof>

<Remark number={1}>
The SLLN requires only $E|X_1| < \infty$, the same condition as the WLLN via characteristic functions
(Theorem 3). The extra strength — a.s. convergence — costs nothing in terms of moment assumptions.
</Remark>

## Almost Sure vs. In Probability: A Separation Example

<Example number={1} title="Moving Bump">
Let the sample space be $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P) = ([0,1], \mathcal{B}([0,1]), \lambda)$ (Lebesgue measure).
For $n \geq 1$ write $n = 2^k + j$ with $0 \leq j < 2^k$, and define
$$X_n(\omega) = \mathbf{1}_{\left[\frac{j}{2^k},\, \frac{j+1}{2^k}\right]}(\omega).$$

Then $E[X_n] = 2^{-k} \to 0$, and for any $\varepsilon \in (0,1)$:
$P(X_n > \varepsilon) = 2^{-k} \to 0$, so $X_n \xrightarrow{P} 0$.

However, for every $\omega \in [0,1]$, every dyadic interval contains $\omega$ infinitely often,
so $X_n(\omega) = 1$ for infinitely many $n$. Hence $X_n(\omega) \not\to 0$ for **every** $\omega$:
the convergence is in probability only, **not** almost surely.
</Example>

## Beyond i.i.d.: Birkhoff's Ergodic Theorem

<Theorem number={5} name="Birkhoff Ergodic Theorem">
Let $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ be a probability space, $T : \Omega \to \Omega$ a measure-preserving
transformation, and $f \in L^1(P)$. Then
$$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} f(T^k \omega) \xrightarrow{\mathrm{a.s.}} E[f \mid \mathcal{I}](\omega)$$
where $\mathcal{I} = \{A \in \mathcal{F} : T^{-1}A = A\}$ is the $\sigma$-algebra of $T$-invariant events.
If $T$ is **ergodic** (i.e., every invariant set has probability 0 or 1), then
$E[f \mid \mathcal{I}] = E[f]$ a.s., recovering the classical LLN for stationary ergodic sequences.
</Theorem>

<Remark number={2}>
The Kolmogorov SLLN is the special case of Birkhoff's theorem where $T$ is the left shift on
$\mathbb{R}^\mathbb{N}$ and $f(x_1, x_2, \ldots) = x_1$.
</Remark>

## Rate of Convergence: Law of the Iterated Logarithm

The LLN tells us that $\bar{X}_n \to \mu$ a.s., but says nothing about *how fast*.

<Theorem number={6} name="Law of the Iterated Logarithm">
Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. with $E[X_1] = \mu$ and $\mathrm{Var}(X_1) = \sigma^2 \in (0, \infty)$.
Then
$$\limsup_{n \to \infty} \frac{\sqrt{n}\,(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sigma\sqrt{2\log\log n}} = 1 \quad \text{a.s.}$$
$$\liminf_{n \to \infty} \frac{\sqrt{n}\,(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sigma\sqrt{2\log\log n}} = -1 \quad \text{a.s.}$$
</Theorem>

<Remark number={3}>
The LIL says the fluctuations of $\bar{X}_n$ are of order $\sqrt{\log\log n / n}$,
which decays slower than $1/\sqrt{n}$ (the CLT scale) but faster than any positive power of $1/n$.
Specifically: the CLT gives distributional behaviour at scale $1/\sqrt{n}$;
the LIL gives the pathwise envelope at scale $\sqrt{\log\log n/n}$.
</Remark>
