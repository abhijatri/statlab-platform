# Central Limit Theorem and Variants

## Classical CLT

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be iid with $E[X_i] = \mu$ and $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 < \infty$. Then:

$$\sqrt{n}\!\left(\bar{X}_n - \mu\right) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \sigma^2)$$

Equivalently, $Z_n = \sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)/\sigma \to N(0,1)$ in distribution.

The key requirements: **finite variance** and **identical distribution**. Both can be relaxed.

## What the CLT says (and doesn't say)

The CLT says the *distribution* of $\bar{X}_n$ approaches normal — it does NOT say individual $X_i$ are normal, that $\bar X_n$ equals a normal variable, or that convergence is uniform across all possible distributions.

## Lindeberg CLT (Non-identically distributed)

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be independent (but not necessarily identically distributed) with $E[X_i] = \mu_i$, $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma_i^2$, and $s_n^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i^2$.

**Lindeberg condition**: for every $\epsilon > 0$,

$$\frac{1}{s_n^2} \sum_{i=1}^n E\!\left[(X_i - \mu_i)^2 \mathbf{1}_{|X_i - \mu_i| > \epsilon s_n}\right] \to 0$$

This says no single summand dominates. Under this condition:

$$\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \mu_i)}{s_n} \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$$

## Lyapunov CLT

The **Lyapunov condition** is sufficient for Lindeberg: if for some $\delta > 0$,

$$\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n E[|X_i - \mu_i|^{2+\delta}]}{s_n^{2+\delta}} \to 0$$

then the CLT holds. This is often easier to verify.

## Multivariate CLT

For iid random vectors $\mathbf{X}_i \in \mathbb{R}^k$ with mean $\boldsymbol\mu$ and covariance matrix $\Sigma$:

$$\sqrt{n}(\bar{\mathbf{X}}_n - \boldsymbol\mu) \xrightarrow{d} N_k(\mathbf{0}, \Sigma)$$

Proof: apply the univariate CLT to $\mathbf{a}^\top \mathbf{X}_i$ for any $\mathbf{a}$, then use the Cramér-Wold device.

## Rate of convergence

The CLT gives convergence without quantifying the error. The Berry-Esseen theorem provides the rate: the Kolmogorov distance between $Z_n$ and $N(0,1)$ is $O(1/\sqrt{n})$ — see the Berry-Esseen concept for details.

## When CLT fails

1. **Infinite variance** (e.g., Cauchy distribution): $\bar X_n$ does not converge to normal; it stays Cauchy.
2. **Heavy dependence**: positively correlated sums may converge to a different limit.
3. **Long-range dependence**: fractional Brownian motion rather than Brownian motion.

## Historical note

The first version was proved by Abraham de Moivre (1733) for Bernoulli random variables. Pierre-Simon Laplace generalized it (1812). The modern proof using characteristic functions is due to Lindeberg (1920) and Lévy (1925).
