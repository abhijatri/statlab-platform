# Berry-Esseen Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem guarantees convergence in distribution but says nothing about *how fast*. The Berry-Esseen theorem gives an explicit, quantitative bound.

## The bound

Let $X_1,\ldots,X_n$ be iid with mean $\mu$, variance $\sigma^2 > 0$, and finite third absolute moment $\rho = E[|X_i - \mu|^3] < \infty$. Then:

$$\sup_{x \in \mathbb{R}} \left| P\!\left(\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar X_n - \mu)}{\sigma} \leq x\right) - \Phi(x) \right| \leq \frac{C \rho}{\sigma^3 \sqrt{n}}$$

where $C$ is a universal constant. The best known value is $C \leq 0.4748$ (Shevtsova, 2010).

## Interpretation

The Kolmogorov distance between $Z_n = \sqrt{n}(\bar X - \mu)/\sigma$ and the standard normal decays as $O(1/\sqrt{n})$. The constant $C\rho/\sigma^3$ measures **skewness**:

- Symmetric distributions ($\rho$ small relative to $\sigma^3$): faster convergence
- Skewed distributions (e.g., exponential): slower convergence, larger constant

## Sharpness

The $O(1/\sqrt{n})$ rate is **optimal**: one cannot get $O(1/n)$ in general. For the Bernoulli distribution with $p = 1/2$:

$$\sup_x |P(Z_n \leq x) - \Phi(x)| \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi n}}$$

which confirms the bound is tight.

## Practical implications

For $n = 100$ and a moderately skewed distribution with $C\rho/\sigma^3 \approx 1$, the error is at most $0.05$ — so the normal approximation has up to 5% error in tail probabilities. At $n = 1000$, this drops to ~1.5%.

This is why bootstrap methods (which do not rely on normal approximation) are preferred for small samples or skewed populations.

## Multivariate Berry-Esseen

For $\mathbf{X}_i \in \mathbb{R}^k$ with covariance $\Sigma$, the uniform bound over convex sets $A$:

$$\sup_A \left|P(\sqrt{n}\bar{\mathbf{X}} \in A) - P(\mathbf{Z} \in A)\right| \leq \frac{C \cdot k^{1/4}}{\sqrt{n}} \cdot \frac{\rho_3}{\lambda_{\min}(\Sigma)^{3/2}}$$

where $\lambda_{\min}$ is the smallest eigenvalue of $\Sigma$. The dimension $k$ enters only through $k^{1/4}$, making the bound useful in moderate dimensions.

## Non-uniform bounds

The Berry-Esseen theorem gives a *uniform* bound over all $x$. For fixed $x$ far in the tail, the actual error can be much smaller — this is the content of the **Petrov bound** and Cramér's large deviation result.

## Historical note

Andrew Berry (1941) and Carl-Gustav Esseen (1942) proved this independently during World War II. Esseen obtained the $C = 7.59$ constant; the constant has been progressively improved over eight decades to the current $0.4748$.
