# Fisher Information

Fisher information measures how sharply the log-likelihood is curved around its maximum — or equivalently, how sensitively a distribution's density changes as the parameter varies.

## Definition via the score function

The **score function** is the gradient of the log-likelihood:

$$s(X; \theta) = \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \log f(X; \theta)$$

The score has a remarkable property: its expected value is **always zero** under the true model, provided regularity conditions hold (interchange of differentiation and integration):

$$E_\theta[s(X;\theta)] = 0$$

Fisher information is the **variance of the score**:

$$I(\theta) = \text{Var}_\theta[s(X;\theta)] = E_\theta\!\left[\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\log f(X;\theta)\right)^{\!2}\right]$$

## Equivalent curvature form

Under mild regularity (twice-differentiable log-likelihood with dominated convergence), Fisher information equals the negative expected second derivative of the log-likelihood:

$$I(\theta) = -E_\theta\!\left[\frac{\partial^2}{\partial\theta^2}\log f(X;\theta)\right]$$

This makes intuitive sense: a sharply curved log-likelihood (large negative curvature) means the data are very informative about θ.

## Examples

**Normal distribution** $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with $\sigma^2$ known:

$$\log f(x;\mu) = -\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} + \text{const}, \quad I(\mu) = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}$$

**Bernoulli** $X \sim \text{Bern}(p)$:

$$I(p) = \frac{1}{p(1-p)}$$

The information diverges as $p \to 0$ or $p \to 1$ — extreme Bernoullis are highly informative.

**Exponential** $X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$:

$$I(\lambda) = \frac{1}{\lambda^2}$$

**Poisson** $X \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$:

$$I(\lambda) = \frac{1}{\lambda}$$

## Additivity for iid samples

For $n$ iid observations $X_1, \ldots, X_n \sim f(\cdot;\theta)$, the joint Fisher information is:

$$I_n(\theta) = n \cdot I(\theta)$$

This additivity is why the Cramér-Rao bound tightens at rate $1/n$.

## Fisher information matrix (multiparameter)

For a parameter vector $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^k$, the **Fisher information matrix** has entries:

$$(I(\theta))_{ij} = E\!\left[\frac{\partial \log f}{\partial \theta_i} \cdot \frac{\partial \log f}{\partial \theta_j}\right] = -E\!\left[\frac{\partial^2 \log f}{\partial \theta_i \, \partial \theta_j}\right]$$

This matrix appears in the multivariate Cramér-Rao bound and in the asymptotic variance of the MLE.

## Historical note

Ronald A. Fisher introduced this quantity in his 1925 paper *Theory of Statistical Estimation*, where he proved that the maximum likelihood estimator is asymptotically efficient. The geometric interpretation — that Fisher information is a Riemannian metric on the space of probability distributions — was developed by Rao (1945) and later became the foundation of **information geometry**.
