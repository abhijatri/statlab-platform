# Maximum Likelihood Estimation

The **maximum likelihood estimator** (MLE) is the parameter value that makes the observed data most probable:

$$\hat\theta_{MLE} = \arg\max_{\theta \in \Theta} L(\theta; \mathbf{x}) = \arg\max_{\theta} \prod_{i=1}^n f(x_i; \theta)$$

In practice, maximize the **log-likelihood** (same argmax, numerically better):

$$\hat\theta = \arg\max_\theta \ell(\theta; \mathbf{x}), \quad \ell(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^n \log f(x_i;\theta)$$

## The score equation

The MLE satisfies the **score equation** (first-order condition):

$$\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\log f(x_i;\hat\theta) = 0$$

For regular families this has a unique solution; for multimodal likelihoods, numerical methods may find local maxima.

## Examples

**Normal** ($\sigma^2$ known): $\hat\mu = \bar{X}$ (sample mean)

**Normal** (both unknown):
$$\hat\mu = \bar{X}, \quad \hat\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i - \bar{X})^2$$

Note: $\hat\sigma^2$ is *biased* (divides by $n$, not $n-1$), but asymptotically negligible.

**Exponential**($\lambda$): $\hat\lambda = 1/\bar{X}$

**Bernoulli**($p$): $\hat p = \bar{X}$

**Poisson**($\lambda$): $\hat\lambda = \bar{X}$

## Asymptotic properties

Under regularity conditions (Wald 1949, Cramér 1946):

**Consistency**: $\hat\theta_n \xrightarrow{p} \theta_0$

**Asymptotic normality**:
$$\sqrt{n}(\hat\theta_n - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} N\!\left(0, I(\theta_0)^{-1}\right)$$

**Asymptotic efficiency**: The MLE achieves the Cramér-Rao bound asymptotically — no consistent estimator can have smaller asymptotic variance.

## Invariance principle

If $\hat\theta$ is the MLE of $\theta$, then $g(\hat\theta)$ is the MLE of $g(\theta)$ for any function $g$. This makes MLEs easy to transform: to estimate the standard deviation, just take the square root of $\hat\sigma^2$.

## Observed vs expected Fisher information

The **expected** Fisher information uses the true parameter: $I(\theta) = E[-\ell''(\theta)]$

The **observed** Fisher information uses the data at the MLE:

$$\hat I = -\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial^2}{\partial\theta^2}\log f(x_i;\hat\theta)$$

Efron and Hinkley (1978) argued the observed information is often more appropriate for finite-sample inference.

## Common misconception

> "MLE is always unbiased."

False. The MLE is biased for finite samples in general. For example, the MLE of $\sigma^2$ in a normal model divides by $n$ (biased), not $n-1$ (unbiased). However, the bias vanishes asymptotically.
