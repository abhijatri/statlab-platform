## Matrices as Geometric Maps

A $2\times 2$ matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}$ acts on every vector
$\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^2$ by matrix multiplication:

$$
A\mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{pmatrix}
= \begin{pmatrix} ax_1 + bx_2 \\ cx_1 + dx_2 \end{pmatrix}
$$

The entire geometry of the map is encoded in the four numbers $a, b, c, d$.

<Intuition>
Instead of thinking about vectors one at a time, think about what happens to the whole plane.
The unit circle becomes an ellipse (possibly degenerate). The unit square becomes a parallelogram.
The two column vectors $\mathbf{e}_1 = (1,0)^T$ and $\mathbf{e}_2 = (0,1)^T$ tell you exactly where
every point lands: column 1 shows where $(1,0)$ goes, column 2 shows where $(0,1)$ goes.
</Intuition>

## Key Quantities

### Determinant — area and orientation

$$
\det(A) = ad - bc
$$

- $|\det A|$ = ratio of output area to input area
- $\det A > 0$: orientation preserved
- $\det A < 0$: orientation reversed (left ↔ right flipped)
- $\det A = 0$: rank-deficient — the plane collapses to a line or point

Try the *Reflect x* preset: $\det = -1$ and the ellipse looks the same size but the unit square
is mirrored.

### Eigenvalues — intrinsic scaling directions

The characteristic equation $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$ gives:

$$
\lambda^2 - \operatorname{tr}(A)\,\lambda + \det(A) = 0
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\lambda = \frac{\operatorname{tr}(A) \pm \sqrt{\operatorname{tr}(A)^2 - 4\det(A)}}{2}
$$

- **Real eigenvalues** ($\operatorname{tr}^2 \ge 4\det$): two directions that are merely scaled (not rotated).
- **Complex eigenvalues** ($\operatorname{tr}^2 < 4\det$): no real fixed direction — the map has an irreducible rotation component.
- $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = \operatorname{tr}(A)$, $\quad\lambda_1\lambda_2 = \det(A)$ — always.

## Connection to the SVD

Every matrix $A$ admits a singular value decomposition $A = U\Sigma V^T$ where $\Sigma$ contains
the **singular values** (semi-axes of the output ellipse). Eigenvalues are the special case where
$U = V$ (i.e., $A$ is symmetric). The visualizer shows the full general case.
