## Three Faces of Stability

Normal, Cauchy, and Lévy are all members of the **stable distribution** family — distributions
that are closed under convolution (sums of i.i.d. copies are the same shape, just rescaled).
The stability index $\alpha$ controls tail weight:

| Distribution | $\alpha$ | Mean | Variance | Tail $P(X>x)$ |
|-------------|----------|------|----------|---------------|
| Normal      | 2        | $\mu$ | $\sigma^2$ | $\sim e^{-x^2/2\sigma^2}$ |
| Cauchy      | 1        | — | — | $\sim \frac{\gamma}{\pi x^2}$ |
| Lévy        | 1/2      | — | — | $\sim \sqrt{\frac{c}{2\pi}}\,x^{-3/2}$ |

<Intuition>
On the PDF plot, all three look like a vaguely bell-shaped bump near the centre.
The dramatic difference is in the tails — use the "Tail P(X > x)" sparkline in the
controls to see this on a log scale.
A Cauchy tail at $x=10$ can be $10^4\times$ heavier than a Normal tail at the same location.
</Intuition>

## Normal Distribution

$$
f(x;\mu,\sigma) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)
$$

All moments are finite. The CLT says that sums of any finite-variance population converge to
this distribution. The tails decay super-exponentially — probabilities beyond $4\sigma$ are
below $10^{-4}$.

## Cauchy Distribution

$$
f(x;x_0,\gamma) = \frac{1}{\pi\gamma\!\left[1+\left(\frac{x-x_0}{\gamma}\right)^2\right]}
$$

The mean integral $\int_{-\infty}^\infty x\,f(x)\,dx$ diverges — not just to $\pm\infty$,
but oscillates. The sample mean of Cauchy i.i.d. draws has the *same* Cauchy distribution
regardless of $n$ — averaging does not help. This makes Cauchy the canonical
"CLT counterexample."

## Lévy Distribution

$$
f(x;\mu,c) = \sqrt{\frac{c}{2\pi}}\,\frac{e^{-c/[2(x-\mu)]}}{(x-\mu)^{3/2}}, \quad x > \mu
$$

One-sided (supported on $(\mu, \infty)$) with a $x^{-3/2}$ power-law tail.
Arises naturally as the first-passage time of a Brownian motion — the time $T$ until a
random walk first hits a positive threshold. Its CDF has the closed form:

$$
F(x;\mu,c) = \operatorname{erfc}\!\left(\sqrt{\frac{c}{2(x-\mu)}}\right)
$$

## Tail Heaviness and Risk

The log-scale tail sparkline in the explorer makes the differences concrete.
A financial loss model using a Normal underestimates $P(\text{loss} > 10\sigma)$ by
many orders of magnitude compared to a Cauchy or Lévy — the 2008 financial crisis
is a canonical example of this misspecification.
