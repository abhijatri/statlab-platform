# Neyman-Pearson Lemma

Consider testing a simple null against a simple alternative:

$$H_0: \theta = \theta_0 \quad \text{vs} \quad H_1: \theta = \theta_1$$

The **Neyman-Pearson Lemma** states that the **likelihood ratio test** is the **most powerful test** of size $\alpha$.

## The likelihood ratio test

Reject $H_0$ if:

$$\Lambda(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{f(\mathbf{x};\theta_1)}{f(\mathbf{x};\theta_0)} > k$$

where $k$ is chosen so that $P(\Lambda(\mathbf{X}) > k \mid H_0) = \alpha$.

## The Lemma (formal statement)

Let $\phi^*$ be the likelihood ratio test with $E_{\theta_0}[\phi^*] = \alpha$. For **any other test** $\phi$ with $E_{\theta_0}[\phi] \leq \alpha$:

$$E_{\theta_1}[\phi^*] \geq E_{\theta_1}[\phi]$$

That is, the likelihood ratio test has the **highest power** among all size-$\alpha$ tests.

## Proof sketch

The proof is elegant. Let $A^* = \{\Lambda > k\}$ be the rejection region. For any other region $A$ with $P(A \mid H_0) \leq P(A^* \mid H_0) = \alpha$:

$$P(A^*|H_1) - P(A|H_1) = \int (1_{A^*} - 1_A)(f_1 - kf_0)\,d\mu \geq 0$$

This follows because $1_{A^*} - 1_A \geq 0$ exactly where $f_1/f_0 > k$, and $\leq 0$ where $f_1/f_0 \leq k$.

## Types of errors

| Decision | $H_0$ true | $H_1$ true |
|---|---|---|
| Reject $H_0$ | **Type I error** (prob $\alpha$) | Correct (power $= 1-\beta$) |
| Accept $H_0$ | Correct | **Type II error** (prob $\beta$) |

There is always a tradeoff: reducing $\alpha$ increases $\beta$.

## Example: Normal means

$X_1,\ldots,X_n \sim N(\mu,1)$, test $\mu=0$ vs $\mu=1$.

$$\log\Lambda = \sum_{i=1}^n \left[\log f(x_i;1) - \log f(x_i;0)\right] = \sum x_i - \frac{n}{2}$$

Reject iff $\bar{X} > z_\alpha/\sqrt{n}$. This is the standard z-test.

## Historical note

Jerzy Neyman and Egon Pearson published the lemma in 1933 in the Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society. Their framework shifted statistics from Fisher's fiducial approach (which generated estimates) to a decision-theoretic framework with controlled error rates. The famous Fisher-Neyman-Pearson controversy over the meaning of hypothesis tests persists in modern statistics.
