## Intuition

<Intuition title="Vertical vs. Horizontal Slicing">
The **Riemann integral** slices the domain vertically: partition $[a,b]$ into sub-intervals
$[x_i, x_{i+1}]$ and approximate $f$ by a constant on each slice.
The approximation is $\sum_i f(\xi_i)(x_{i+1} - x_i)$.

The **Lebesgue integral** slices the *range* horizontally: instead of asking
"how large is $f$ on this domain interval?", ask
"on what set does $f$ take values near $y$?", and weight by the *measure* of that set.

The formal construction builds this up from **simple functions** —
finite linear combinations of indicator functions of measurable sets.
For nonneg. $f$, the Lebesgue integral is the supremum of integrals of all simple functions
lying below $f$.

The payoff is dramatic: Lebesgue integration handles functions that are "too irregular" for Riemann,
and — crucially — it justifies exchanging limits and integrals under much weaker conditions.
This limit-interchange is the engine of probability theory and functional analysis.
</Intuition>

## Measure Spaces

<Definition number={1} name="σ-Algebra">
A collection $\mathcal{F} \subseteq 2^\Omega$ of subsets of a set $\Omega$ is a **$\sigma$-algebra** if:
1. $\Omega \in \mathcal{F}$,
2. $A \in \mathcal{F} \implies A^c \in \mathcal{F}$ (closed under complements),
3. $A_1, A_2, \ldots \in \mathcal{F} \implies \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty A_n \in \mathcal{F}$ (closed under countable unions).

The pair $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ is called a **measurable space**.
</Definition>

<Definition number={2} name="Measure">
A function $\mu : \mathcal{F} \to [0, \infty]$ on a $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{F}$ is a **measure** if:
1. $\mu(\emptyset) = 0$,
2. (**$\sigma$-additivity**) For pairwise disjoint $A_1, A_2, \ldots \in \mathcal{F}$:
$$\mu\!\left(\bigsqcup_{n=1}^\infty A_n\right) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \mu(A_n).$$

The triple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ is a **measure space**. If $\mu(\Omega) = 1$, it is a **probability space**.
</Definition>

<Definition number={3} name="Measurable Function">
A function $f : (\Omega, \mathcal{F}) \to (\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$ is **measurable** if
$$f^{-1}(B) = \{\omega \in \Omega : f(\omega) \in B\} \in \mathcal{F}$$
for every Borel set $B \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$. Equivalently: $\{f > t\} \in \mathcal{F}$ for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$.
</Definition>

<Remark number={1}>
It suffices to check $\{f > t\} \in \mathcal{F}$ for all rational $t$, since the Borel sets
are generated by open rays $(t, \infty)$.
</Remark>

## Simple Functions and the Integral

<Definition number={4} name="Simple Function">
A measurable function $\varphi : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ is **simple** if it takes only finitely many values.
Its **canonical representation** is
$$\varphi = \sum_{i=1}^k a_i \mathbf{1}_{A_i}$$
where $a_1, \ldots, a_k$ are the distinct nonzero values of $\varphi$ and $A_i = \varphi^{-1}(\{a_i\}) \in \mathcal{F}$.
</Definition>

<Definition number={5} name="Integral of a Simple Function">
For a nonneg. simple function $\varphi = \sum_{i=1}^k a_i \mathbf{1}_{A_i}$ (with $a_i \geq 0$), its
**Lebesgue integral** with respect to $\mu$ is
$$\int \varphi \, d\mu = \sum_{i=1}^k a_i \,\mu(A_i) \in [0, \infty].$$
Using the convention $0 \cdot \infty = 0$.
</Definition>

<Lemma number={1} name="Well-Definedness">
The value $\int \varphi \, d\mu$ is independent of the representation of $\varphi$ as a linear combination of indicators.
</Lemma>

<Proof collapsible defaultOpen={false} label="Proof of Lemma 1">
Let $\varphi = \sum_i a_i \mathbf{1}_{A_i} = \sum_j b_j \mathbf{1}_{B_j}$ be two representations.
Since $A_i = \bigsqcup_j (A_i \cap B_j)$ and on $A_i \cap B_j$ the value of $\varphi$ is both $a_i$ and $b_j$, we have $a_i = b_j$ whenever $\mu(A_i \cap B_j) > 0$.

Therefore:
$$\sum_i a_i \mu(A_i) = \sum_i a_i \sum_j \mu(A_i \cap B_j) = \sum_{i,j} a_i \mu(A_i \cap B_j)$$
$$= \sum_{i,j} b_j \mu(A_i \cap B_j) = \sum_j b_j \sum_i \mu(A_i \cap B_j) = \sum_j b_j \mu(B_j). \qquad\square$$

(We used $a_i = b_j$ on $A_i \cap B_j$ to switch the coefficient.)
</Proof>

## Integration of Nonneg. Measurable Functions

<Definition number={6} name="Lebesgue Integral (Nonneg. Case)">
For a **nonneg. measurable** function $f : \Omega \to [0, \infty]$, the **Lebesgue integral** is
$$\int f \, d\mu = \sup\left\{\int \varphi \, d\mu : \varphi \text{ simple},\; 0 \leq \varphi \leq f\right\} \in [0, \infty].$$
</Definition>

<Remark number={2} title="Monotone Approximation">
By the **simple function approximation theorem**, for any nonneg. measurable $f$ there exists
a sequence of simple functions $\varphi_n \uparrow f$ pointwise. The integral is therefore
also $\lim_{n \to \infty} \int \varphi_n \, d\mu$.
</Remark>

<Theorem number={1} name="Monotone Convergence Theorem (MCT)">
Let $(f_n)_{n \geq 1}$ be a sequence of nonneg. measurable functions with
$f_n(\omega) \uparrow f(\omega)$ for $\mu$-a.e.\ $\omega$. Then
$$\int f \, d\mu = \lim_{n \to \infty} \int f_n \, d\mu.$$
</Theorem>

<Proof label="Proof of MCT">
**Upper bound.** Since $f_n \leq f$, monotonicity of the integral gives
$\int f_n \, d\mu \leq \int f \, d\mu$ for all $n$, hence $L := \lim_n \int f_n \, d\mu \leq \int f \, d\mu$.

**Lower bound.** Fix $\alpha \in (0, 1)$ and let $\varphi$ be any simple function with
$0 \leq \varphi \leq f$. Define the sets
$$A_n = \bigl\{\omega : f_n(\omega) \geq \alpha\,\varphi(\omega)\bigr\}, \qquad n \geq 1.$$

Since $f_n \uparrow f \geq \varphi > \alpha\varphi$ (where $\varphi > 0$), we have $A_n \uparrow \Omega$.
Therefore, using monotonicity and the definition of $A_n$:
$$\int f_n \, d\mu \;\geq\; \int_{A_n} f_n \, d\mu \;\geq\; \alpha \int_{A_n} \varphi \, d\mu.$$

As $n \to \infty$, $A_n \uparrow \Omega$ and by $\sigma$-additivity (continuity of measure from below),
$\int_{A_n} \varphi \, d\mu \to \int \varphi \, d\mu$.

Hence $L \geq \alpha \int \varphi \, d\mu$.
Since $\alpha < 1$ was arbitrary, $L \geq \int \varphi \, d\mu$.
Since $\varphi \leq f$ was arbitrary, taking the supremum over $\varphi$ gives $L \geq \int f \, d\mu$.

Combining both directions: $L = \int f \, d\mu$.
</Proof>

## General Integrable Functions

<Definition number={7} name="Positive and Negative Parts">
For any measurable $f : \Omega \to [-\infty, \infty]$, define:
$$f^+ = \max(f, 0), \qquad f^- = \max(-f, 0).$$
Both $f^+$ and $f^-$ are nonneg. measurable, and $f = f^+ - f^-$, $|f| = f^+ + f^-$.
</Definition>

<Definition number={8} name="Lebesgue Integral (General Case)">
A measurable function $f$ is **Lebesgue integrable** (written $f \in L^1(\mu)$) if
$\int f^+ \, d\mu < \infty$ and $\int f^- \, d\mu < \infty$, equivalently $\int |f| \, d\mu < \infty$.
In that case:
$$\int f \, d\mu = \int f^+ \, d\mu - \int f^- \, d\mu \in \mathbb{R}.$$
</Definition>

<Warning title="Signed Infinite Integrals">
If $\int f^+ \, d\mu = \int f^- \, d\mu = \infty$, the expression $\infty - \infty$ is not defined.
A function for which only one part is infinite can be assigned an extended value $(\pm\infty)$,
but the standard $L^1$ space requires both parts to be finite.
</Warning>

## Convergence Theorems

The following three theorems are the core tools of measure-theoretic analysis and probability.

<Theorem number={2} name="Fatou's Lemma">
Let $(f_n)$ be a sequence of nonneg. measurable functions. Then
$$\int \liminf_{n \to \infty} f_n \, d\mu \;\leq\; \liminf_{n \to \infty} \int f_n \, d\mu.$$
</Theorem>

<Proof label="Proof of Fatou's Lemma">
Let $g_n = \inf_{k \geq n} f_k$. Then $g_n$ is measurable, $g_n \uparrow \liminf f_n$,
and $g_n \leq f_k$ for all $k \geq n$.

By MCT: $\int \liminf f_n \, d\mu = \lim_n \int g_n \, d\mu$.

For each $n$ and $k \geq n$: $\int g_n \, d\mu \leq \int f_k \, d\mu$, so
$\int g_n \, d\mu \leq \inf_{k \geq n} \int f_k \, d\mu$.

Taking $n \to \infty$: $\lim_n \int g_n \, d\mu \leq \lim_n \inf_{k \geq n} \int f_k \, d\mu = \liminf \int f_n \, d\mu$.
</Proof>

<Theorem number={3} name="Dominated Convergence Theorem (DCT)">
Let $f_n \to f$ pointwise $\mu$-a.e., and suppose there exists a **dominating function**
$g \in L^1(\mu)$ with $|f_n(\omega)| \leq g(\omega)$ for $\mu$-a.e.\ $\omega$ and all $n$.
Then $f \in L^1(\mu)$ and
$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \int f_n \, d\mu = \int f \, d\mu.$$
Equivalently, $\|f_n - f\|_1 = \int |f_n - f| \, d\mu \to 0$.
</Theorem>

<Proof label="Proof of DCT via Fatou">
Since $|f_n| \leq g$ and $g \in L^1$, all functions $f_n$ and their limit $f$ are in $L^1$.

**Step 1** ($\limsup \leq$): Apply Fatou's lemma to the nonneg. sequence $g - f_n \geq 0$:
$$\int (g - f) \, d\mu \leq \liminf_{n}\int (g - f_n)\, d\mu = \int g \, d\mu - \limsup_n \int f_n \, d\mu.$$
Cancelling $\int g \, d\mu$ (finite!) gives $\limsup_n \int f_n \, d\mu \leq \int f \, d\mu$.

**Step 2** ($\liminf \geq$): Apply Fatou's lemma to $g + f_n \geq 0$:
$$\int (g + f) \, d\mu \leq \liminf_n \int (g + f_n) \, d\mu = \int g \, d\mu + \liminf_n \int f_n \, d\mu.$$
Cancelling $\int g \, d\mu$ gives $\liminf_n \int f_n \, d\mu \geq \int f \, d\mu$.

**Combining:** $\int f \, d\mu \leq \liminf \int f_n \leq \limsup \int f_n \leq \int f \, d\mu$,
so $\lim_n \int f_n \, d\mu = \int f \, d\mu$.
</Proof>

## Comparison with Riemann Integration

<Theorem number={4} name="Riemann implies Lebesgue">
Let $f : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be Riemann integrable. Then $f$ is Lebesgue integrable
with respect to Lebesgue measure $\lambda$, and the two integrals agree:
$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx \;=\; \int_{[a,b]} f \, d\lambda.$$
</Theorem>

<Example number={1} title="A Function Integrable Only in the Lebesgue Sense">
Let $f = \mathbf{1}_{\mathbb{Q} \cap [0,1]}$, the indicator of the rationals in $[0,1]$.

**Riemann integral does not exist:** every sub-interval contains both rationals and irrationals,
so any Riemann sum oscillates between 0 and 1. Upper and lower Darboux integrals are 1 and 0.

**Lebesgue integral is 0:** $\mathbb{Q}$ is countable, hence $\lambda(\mathbb{Q} \cap [0,1]) = 0$, so
$\int f \, d\lambda = 1 \cdot \lambda(\mathbb{Q}) + 0 \cdot \lambda([0,1] \setminus \mathbb{Q}) = 0$.
</Example>

<Remark number={3}>
The Lebesgue criterion for Riemann integrability states: a bounded function $f : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$
is Riemann integrable if and only if it is continuous **Lebesgue almost everywhere**.
The indicator of the rationals fails this criterion at every point.
</Remark>
