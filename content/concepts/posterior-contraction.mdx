# Posterior Contraction Rates

In the parametric Bernstein-von Mises theorem, the posterior concentrates at rate $1/\sqrt{n}$. In nonparametric models, the truth $\theta_0$ lives in an infinite-dimensional space and the rate depends on the prior's ability to put mass near $\theta_0$.

## Definition

The posterior contracts around $\theta_0$ at rate $\varepsilon_n$ (with $\varepsilon_n \to 0$) if for any $M_n \to \infty$:

$$\Pi\!\left(d(\theta, \theta_0) > M_n \varepsilon_n \;\middle|\; X_1, \ldots, X_n\right) \xrightarrow{P_{\theta_0}} 0$$

where $d$ is an appropriate metric (Hellinger, total variation, $L^2$, etc.).

## Ghosal-Ghosh-van der Vaart Framework

The key theorem gives sufficient conditions on the prior and model. Let $\Theta_n$ be a sieve (growing subset of $\Theta$). The posterior contracts at rate $\varepsilon_n$ if:

**Condition 1 (Prior mass)**: The prior assigns enough mass near the truth:
$$\Pi(\theta: d_{KL}(\theta_0, \theta) \leq \varepsilon_n^2, V(\theta_0,\theta) \leq \varepsilon_n^2) \geq e^{-n\varepsilon_n^2}$$

where $V$ is the second KL divergence moment.

**Condition 2 (Complexity)**: The sieve is not too complex:
$$\log N(\varepsilon_n, \Theta_n, d) \leq n\varepsilon_n^2$$

(metric entropy of the sieve with $\varepsilon_n$-balls).

**Condition 3 (Prior on complement)**: $\Pi(\Theta_n^c) \leq e^{-4n\varepsilon_n^2}$.

## Parametric case

In a $k$-dimensional parametric model:
- Prior mass condition: satisfied for any $\theta_0$ in the interior if prior is continuous — gives $\varepsilon_n^2 \asymp 1/n$, so $\varepsilon_n \asymp 1/\sqrt{n}$.
- This recovers the Bernstein-von Mises rate.

## Gaussian process prior

For nonparametric regression $Y_i = f(x_i) + \varepsilon_i$ with $f$ in a Sobolev space $H^\alpha$ (smoothness $\alpha$) and a GP prior with Matérn covariance:

$$\varepsilon_n \asymp n^{-\alpha/(2\alpha + d)}$$

This matches the **minimax rate** for Sobolev-$\alpha$ functions — the posterior is rate-adaptive.

## Adaptive rates via hyperpriors

Putting a prior on the bandwidth/lengthscale of the GP prior enables **adaptation**: the posterior automatically achieves the optimal rate even when $\alpha$ is unknown. This is one of the major advantages of Bayesian nonparametrics over plug-in frequentist methods.

## Credible sets and coverage

Unlike parametric BvM, nonparametric credible intervals may not have correct frequentist coverage. For adaptive priors, credible intervals at one smoothness may under-cover at other smoothnesses. Szabo et al. (2015) showed this fundamental limitation: adaptive Bayesian credible sets cannot have uniform coverage guarantees.
