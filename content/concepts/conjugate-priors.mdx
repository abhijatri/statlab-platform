# Conjugate Priors

A prior $\pi(\theta)$ is **conjugate** to a likelihood $f(\mathbf{x}|\theta)$ if the posterior $\pi(\theta|\mathbf{x}) \propto f(\mathbf{x}|\theta)\pi(\theta)$ belongs to the same parametric family as the prior.

Conjugacy enables exact, closed-form posterior computation — no MCMC needed.

## Beta-Binomial Conjugacy

**Likelihood**: $X \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$, so $f(x|p) \propto p^x(1-p)^{n-x}$

**Prior**: $p \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$, so $\pi(p) \propto p^{\alpha-1}(1-p)^{\beta-1}$

**Posterior**:
$$p | x \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + x, \;\beta + n - x)$$

The prior pseudo-counts $(\alpha, \beta)$ add directly to the observed counts $(x, n-x)$.

**Posterior mean**: $\hat p = (\alpha + x)/(\alpha + \beta + n)$ — a weighted average of the prior mean $\alpha/(\alpha+\beta)$ and MLE $x/n$.

## Gamma-Poisson Conjugacy

**Likelihood**: $X_1,\ldots,X_n \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$

**Prior**: $\lambda \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$

**Posterior**:
$$\lambda | \mathbf{x} \sim \text{Gamma}\!\left(\alpha + n\bar x,\; \beta + n\right)$$

## Normal-Normal Conjugacy

**Likelihood**: $X_1,\ldots,X_n \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ ($\sigma^2$ known)

**Prior**: $\mu \sim N(\mu_0, \tau_0^2)$

**Posterior**: $\mu | \mathbf{x} \sim N(\mu_1, \tau_1^2)$ where:

$$\frac{1}{\tau_1^2} = \frac{1}{\tau_0^2} + \frac{n}{\sigma^2}, \quad \mu_1 = \tau_1^2\left(\frac{\mu_0}{\tau_0^2} + \frac{n\bar x}{\sigma^2}\right)$$

The posterior precision (reciprocal variance) is the **sum** of prior and data precisions.

## General exponential family conjugate

For $f(x|\eta) = h(x)\exp(\eta T(x) - A(\eta))$, the conjugate prior is:

$$\pi(\eta; \tau, n_0) \propto \exp\!\left(\eta^\top \tau - n_0 A(\eta)\right)$$

The posterior is $\pi(\eta; \tau + \sum T(x_i), n_0 + n)$ — additive update.

## Prior strength interpretation

The hyperparameters can be interpreted as "prior data": a $\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ prior corresponds to having previously observed $\alpha - 1$ successes and $\beta - 1$ failures. As $n \to \infty$, the prior is overwhelmed by the data — a formal statement of this is the Bernstein-von Mises theorem.

## Improper priors

The uniform prior $\pi(p) = 1$ on $[0,1]$ is $\text{Beta}(1,1)$ — a conjugate prior with unit pseudo-counts. The Jeffreys prior $\pi(p) \propto [p(1-p)]^{-1/2}$ is $\text{Beta}(1/2, 1/2)$, chosen to be invariant under reparametrization.
